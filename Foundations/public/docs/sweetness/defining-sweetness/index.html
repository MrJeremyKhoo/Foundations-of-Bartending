    Defining Sweetness: Culturally

    In the early 17th century, a small, unassuming plant began its journey from the lush fields of the Caribbean to the bustling markets of Europe. This plant, Saccharum officinarum, known to us as sugarcane, would not only sweeten our foods but also stir the currents of history, culture, and human behavior. To understand the profound impact of sweetness, we must first trace its path through time and society.

    Before Sugar link

    Sweetness is a perception

    Before widespead availability of sugar, we still craved sweetness in drinks.Early Beverages were crafted with natural sweetness from fruits, herbs and spices. Drinks like mead and ale from the Europe were case in point: Honey, water, sometimes fruits or spices were the ingredients.

    In the Middle ease beverages such as sharbat were made by infusing water with fruit juices and herb.

    In Asia, Beverages like tea and rice-based wines were common. In America the indigenous people crafted tula-pah, a corn beer made by fermenting sprouted corn kenels flavoured with herbs and roots.

    When sugar came around, it did alter existing recipies, however the roots and culture from which modern drinks appear are still very much present today.

    Sugar trade link

    The 17th century was a world where sugar was as rare and precious as gold.As a luxury commodity, sugar was reserved for the elite and used sparingly. The insatiable European demand for sugar led to the establishment of vast plantations in the Caribbean, manned by enslaved Africans. This dark chapter in history not only fueled the transatlantic slave trade but also laid the groundwork for the modern global economy. The sugar trade was more than an economic venture; it was a catalyst for social upheaval and cultural exchange.

    Sugar Status link

    As sugar became more accessible, it transitioned from a symbol of wealth to a staple in the average household. Yet, its association with status persisted.

    In Victorian England, the ability to host a tea party with an array of sweet treats was a mark of social standing. The act of sharing sweets became a ritual, reinforcing social bonds and hierarchies.

    This phenomenon wasn’t confined to England. Across the globe, from the opulent feasts of the Mughal Empire to the communal desserts of Japan, sweetness served as a social glue, binding communities together while subtly delineating class distinctions.

    Why we crave Sweetness? link

    The scarcity at the time meant that only the privileged could indulge in sweet and perhaps only on special occasion. We still see reminence of the practise today. As children, many of us experienced the familiar ritual: a parent or caregiver offering a sugary treat as a reward for good behavior or academic achievement.

    Combined with our biological predisposition to seek out sweetness. The craving for sweetness have become deeply ingrained.

    However, health-conscious individuals may experience a slight taming of these cravings.
